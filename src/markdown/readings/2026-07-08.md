The first of two volumes (the second being "From Third World to First"), this book chronicles the autobiographer's life from childhood during a dark WWII Japanese occupation, through legal and political careers guiding Singapore's merger into Malaysia, and its soon after split for independence in 1965.

My interest in Singapore began from a colleague who resigned to migrate there for his career. I later learned its founding father is widely hailed as one of the greatest statesmen to have lived. Even into his elderly years, people from around the world constantly sought his diplomatic insights.

This volume covers the author's efforts to lay the foundations for a first world nation by fighting off the corruptions that would otherwise have kept it third world, within a political web that could spell his own incarceration or death with one wrong move.

I summarised key events, terminology, and people, followed by some political strategy and other lessons.



## Context

### Key events

* **15th February - 13th September 1942 Japanese Occupation**: During Singapore's occupation in World War II, the author narrowly escaped execution by the Japanese army's purging of ethnically Chinese men. After the war, Britain sought to de-colonise the region.

* **1946 - 1950 British Studies**: The author studied law in London then Cambridge with his wife. Initially enamoured by the political left's social welfare system in Britain, he later saw past his own idealism and saw it as too much of a burden for any nation. He turned to the realistic first principle that individuals need to strive for themselves, as that was *"...the driving force for progress throughout human evolution"* (p.129).

* **1950 - 1959 Legal Practice**: The author returned to Singapore to practice law, advising many races whilst building the People's Action Party (PAP) in 1954 as an opposition coalition comprised of his non-communist allies, and of pro-communist members. The author first meets the underground communist leader he dubbed as "the Plen" in March 1958.

* **5th June 1959**: The author is sworn in as prime minister at age 35 upon the PAP's general election win.

* **July 1960 - July 1961 PAP Split**: Unions pushed for the PAP to become more communist, despite PAP mobilising grassroots non-communist support to counter them. The author pushed for a Legislative Assembly vote of confidence that split the pro-communist PAP assemblymen off into their own Barisan Sosialis party, leaving the PAP with an uncompromised team of non-communists.

* **September 1961 - September 1962 Malaysian Merge**: The author gave a series of nationwide multilingual radio talks titled the "Battle for Merger" to gain public support for merging with the Malaysian federation. The author exposed the pro-communist conspiracy including the existence of "the Plen" (p.665). A Singaporean referendum votes to merge with Malaysia.

* **16th September 1963 Indonesian Confrontation**: The Malaysian federation comprising Malaya, Sarawak, and Sabah is formed. Singapore is reluctantly taken in by Malaya's Tunku (prince) due to British pressure. This triggers neighbouring rival Indonesia to mount military raids and bombings against Malaysia. Britain, Australia, and New Zealand, deploy their military to defend Malaysia.

* **July - September 1964 Racial Riots**: The merger also triggers the convention of 123 Muslim bodies, sponsored by the United Malays National Organisation (UMNO). Led by UMNO secretary-general Ja'afar Albar, they pursued the author's removal from office by instigating racial division, communal violence, and killings in Singapore between ethnic Malays and Chinese.

* **January - July 1965 Eve of Independence**: The author pushed for an ethnically equal "Malaysian Malaysia" via the PAP-backed Malaysian Solidarity Convention (MSC) of non-communal federal opposition parties. Impasse between the Tunku and UMNO's incumbent Malay dominion, versus the PAP and MSC's push for racial equality, led to the Tunku's decision to expel Singapore from Malaysia. Malaysia's constitution was secretly amended to subvert Western prevention of the split, and the amendment passed through Malaysian parliament in one urgent sitting.

* **9th August 1965 Singapore Independence**: The author and PAP leave their MSC allies on the Malaysian peninsula behind.

* **May 1969 Kuala Lumpur Riots**: Malaysian election saw the loss, but nevertheless the growth, of opposition parties against the Malayan incumbents. Celebration of this growth triggered communal violence, resulting in hundreds of ethnic Chinese in Malaysia being maimed and killed.

### Key terminology

* **Chauvinism**: Excessively irrational belief in the superiority of one's own group. In this book it refers to ethnic or racial chauvinism (Merriam Webster).

* **Socialism**: An ideology where major industries or resources are owned or regulated collectively by the state (Merriam Webster).

* **Communism**: An ideology where society is classless, property is collectively owned, and the state is withered away  (Merriam Webster).

* **Communalism** *(general definition)*: Relates to a community, shared ownership, shared responsibility, or collective decision‑making (Merriam Webster).

* **Communalism** *(Malayan-Chinese riot contextualised definition)*: Politics based on ethnic loyalty where a group politically advances its own racial interests at the expense of other communities.

* **Non-communalism** *(Malayan-Chinese riot contextualised political definition)*: Politics based on equal citizenship rather than ethnic or racial identity.

* **Crypto‑communist**: Someone who secretly supports communism.

* **Crypto‑anti‑communist**: Someone who secretly opposes communism.

**Spoiler**: Many stakeholders suspected the author to be a crypto-communist, but he cites that William Goode correctly identified him as a crypto-anti-communist (p.382).

### Key organisations and people

**Non-communist and non-communalist (ethnically diverse)**

* **People's Action Party (PAP)**: Singapore-based. Branched into Malayan peninsula after *Malaysian Merge*, but withdrew upon *Singapore Independence*.
  - Goh Keng Swee, *"...a first-class mind ...too intellectual and not susceptible to persuasion or temptation ...he was my alter ego, always the sceptic, always turning a proposition on its head to reveal its flaws and help me reshape it. He was my resident intellectual par excellence and a doughty fighter"*, and came up with the countermoves for every clever communist move (p.43, 462 & 510).

  - Sinnathamby Rajaratnam (a.k.a. Raja), with an unwavering fighting spirit, robustness, conviction of PAP being right, and debunking communist attacks, *"Raja roared like a lion"* (p.510).

  - Ong Pang Boon, quiet and soft-spoken, dependable and reliable, good in assessing PAP loyalists, keeping them together, and good of heart (p.510).

  - Toh Chin Chye, *"...a science student one year my senior, hardworking, systematic, quiet and consistent"* (p.43).

* **Malaysian Solidarity Convention (MSC)**: Malaya-based. Stood for a non-communal Malaysia alongside the PAP (p.17).

* **Singapore's Special Branch**: Intelligence unit. Owned by Britain before WWII, commanded by Singapore with British advisors post-WWII.

**Pro-communist (predominantly Chinese)**

* **Malayan Communist Front (MCF)**: Malaya-based. Expanded into Singapore via the CUF and Communist Underground.

* **Communist United Front (CUF)**: Singapore-based. Legal above ground operations included trade unions, student groups, and cultural associations.

* **Communist Underground**: Singapore-based. *"...a secret network of disciplined cadets grouped in cells of about four, each with a leader"* (p.174).
  - Fang Chuang Pi (a.k.a. the "Plen"), underground Chinese communist leader.

* **Barisan Sosialis (BS)**: Singapore-based. Comprised of pro-communist cofounding PAP members.
  - Lim Chin Siong, named the main communist enemy of the PAP (p.375).

**Pro-communalists (predominantly Malayan, a.k.a. "Ultras")**

* **United Malays National Organisation (UMNO)**: Founded by Tunku. The ruling party of the Alliance coalition that governed Malaya (p.17).
  - Tunku Abdul Rahman (a.k.a. Tunku), "Tunku" means prince of Malaysia but he also served as prime minister, *"...he liked to spend time strolling through the Burlington Arcade near the Ritz to buy fancy waistcoats or handkerchiefs as he had done in his misspent youth in England"* (p.443). Personally, this is a pastime I very much approve of.

  - Tun Abdul Razak bin Hussein (a.k.a. Razak), with "Tun" being amongst the highest civilian titles, and he was deputy prime minister handling all the details as *"...a capable, hardworking and meticulous man"* (p.411).

* Convention of 123 Muslim bodies (remained as an unnamed singular entity in the book), backed by the UMNO.

* **Utusan Melayu** and **Malayan Merdeka**: Newspapers published in Malay language across the Malayan peninsula and Singapore, which spread communal hate leading to the *Racial Riots*.

**Anti-communist**

* **Malaysian Chinese Association (MCA)**: Malaya-based. Predominantly ethnically Chinese who support the political dominance of Malayans. Alliance member. Strongly UMNO-allied. Political enemy of PAP, because the PAP are more popular and not as aggressive against pro-communists.
  - Tan Siew Sin (party president) blocked and siphoned Singapore's economic progress during its inclusion within Malaysia (p.600).

**Colonialist**

* William Goode, Chief Secretary of the Legislative assembly, a quietly spoken but steely person, who *"...laughed easily and had a bluff manner"* (p.197).

* Anthony Head, British High Commissioner to Malaysia, a top-ranking political heavyweight whose assessments to London on the tussle between the PAP and Malayan Ultras crucially impacted Singapore and Malaysia's history (p.519-520).

* Philip Moore, Deputy British High Commissioner during the decolonisation of Singapore.

* Anthony Greenwood, shadow cabinet minister for colonial affairs in charge of the Labour party's International Department (p.572).

**Anti-colonialist**

* David Marshall, initially a major but later a minor political opponent to the PAP. He *"...was extremely anxious to live up to his self-perceived role as a liberal and a socialist bent on freeing Singapore from colonialism"*, and he was overly idealistic and loud (p.197 & 236).

## BOOK NOTES

### PERSONAL INTEGRITY

* **Internalise the right attitude**: Build a strong personal foundation before trying to change the world, like internally promising to make people think differently of you through your future life achievements, rather than verbally retaliate during non-consequential situations (p.231).

* **Pursue integrity**: Avoid situations that could be leveraged against you. But understand there is a trade-off in always acting constitutionally and pursuing integrity, as others who value the opposite, may perceive such rigidness as a threat (p.657). So you also need cunning to survive.

* **Keep your word**: Even if it shoots you in the foot, keeping your word can pay off long-term if you do it strategically and publicly, as it can help build perceptions of your trustworthiness (p.308).

* **Manage your public image**: Do not concede your image to propagandistic media. Ensure your name or the groups to which you belong are named such they reflect your stance (p.142).

### ESTABLISH A CORE MISSION

* **Clearly define your goal and the role you serve toward it**: The author did so for himself in this quote:

  *"The people as a whole must have self-respect and the will to strive to make a nation of themselves. The task of the leaders must be to provide or create for them a strong framework within which they can learn, work hard, be productive and be rewarded accordingly"* (p.132).

* **Communicate the mission**: By instilling a rational humility in yourself and others to build pragmatic expectations and effort toward that goal.

  *"The good things of life do not fall from the skies. They can only come by hard work and over a long time. The government cannot produce results unless the people support and sustain the work of the government... There may be times when, in the interests of the whole community, we may have to take steps that are unpopular with a section of the community. On such occasions, remember that the principle which guides our actions is that the paramount interest of the whole community must prevail"* (p.309).

* **Stay loyal to your core mission**: Even if you need to give up a bigger opportunity, do not stray from your core path, like how the author forfeited the greater "Malaysian Malaysia" dream to protect his core mission of building Singapore (p.644).

### DEVELOP PERSONAL POLITICAL SKILLS

* **Control your emotions**: Especially during difficult times when your own outward morale impacts the people's morale, which would be needed to overcome those times (p.575).

* **Be self-aware**: When relationships between countries change, so do the perceptions and expectations other leaders may have of you. You are always free to disregard these changes to pursue your country's interests, even if it impacts subsequent diplomacy (p.417).

* **Be decisionally far-sighted, not emotionally short-sighted**: Giving into short-term emotions sacrifices long-term interests. For example:

  - Lim Yew Hock cleaned up rioting Chinese-educated students with a hard anti-communist approach making him look like a colonialist tool. The author outlined a better idea of obligating parents to collect their children, then let Special Branch catch the remaining leaders (p.250).

  - Tan Siew Sin drove Singapore out of Malaysia to secure his position as the federation's leader of ethnic Chinese. Despite achieving that, he resigned from the Malaysian Chinese Association following hundreds of Chinese deaths in the *Kuala Lumpur Riots* (p.642-643).

* **Read extensively**: Like the author, frequently read new information sources as you climb the political ladder, so you can plan and act sooner.

* **Build a surveillance network**: Politics is information warfare. Have weekly meetings with your law enforcement and intelligence branches, as surveillance information may help in important negotiations and debates (p.374, 518).

* **Do not take media at face value**: Media stances have subtle signals the public ignores. For example, if a newspaper prints views known as unacceptable to its government, it implies that outlet may have foreign government backing (p.463-464).

* **Pay attention for implied intentions**: During a hearing on the *Racial Riots*, Razak told Goh Keng Swee that having the PAP in the federal cabinet without the author would avoid further conflict, and indirectly admitted his control over news media. This implied the pro-communal intention was to remove the author from office (p.568-569).

* **Pay attention for misaligned actions**: There are always external motives willing to subvert you. For example:
  - Perceived allies may meet with your political enemies to achieve their own ends (p.378).
  - When someone shows off their presence at a place intended for secrecy, it signals their intention to betray (p.604).

* **Publicly display trust in others**: Use opportunities to show trust. Even if you believe someone is facilitating a problem, publicly saying you trust them to do the right thing, puts them into a position to reciprocate (p.564).

* **Learn how your constituency thinks**: Then guide them to the desired goal. For example: *"The Chinese-speaking in Singapore, like the Chinese-speaking everywhere in Southeast Asia, traditionally preferred to sit on the fence until they saw clearly which way the wind was blowing... So they would even support a government that they knew was being manipulated by the communists, if the communists looked like winning in the long run"* (p.390). So the author made the pro-communists look like they were not winning, to win the ethnic Chinese public to his side.

### CONSTANTLY BUILD YOUR NETWORK

* **Build a reputation of discretion**: For example, the author subtly hinted to an overseas diplomatic counterpart that race riot agitators in Singapore had "high-level connections" as his reason for not rushing back home, conferring a respectable image of cool-headed decision-making (p.572).

* **Appreciate rational and logical people**: Regardless if they are friend or foe. For example, the author made these praises:
  - Trevor Thomas, *"My best supervisor...had a crisp, clear, methodical mind"*.
  - William S. Thatcher's writing was intelligent, mature, sensible, with a touch of dry humour (p.112).
  - Lim Chin Seong *"...was modest, humble and well-behaved, with a dedication to the cause that won my admiration and respect ...totally loyal, absolutely dependable, always ready to execute orders to the best of his ability ...He knew I was not a communist and I knew he was one. And we accepted each other as such"* (p.233).

* **Acknowledge others to be acknowledged yourself**: All relationships begin with your initiative to introduce yourself. For example, before the *Malaysian Merge*, the author toured non-aligned nations to garner support for Malaysia, but also to create personal connections (p.427).

* **Be civil and courteous**: Even with potential enemies, though it takes a subtler form with the underground. The author adopted a pseudonym showing he knew the Plen's identity, but did not try to arrest him. He was also careful not to commit anything to the Plen which he may later need to go back on his word on (p.357-360).

* **Be open to today's enemy being tomorrow's ally**: People can grow up and change their minds (p.158). The author identified and took under his wing youth who got swept into the pro-communist movement later realising its ruthlessness then left it, like a former pro-communist newspaper employee who became his press secretary (p.473-474).

* **Ask for help**: Being public about your efforts makes you a visible potential ally, and raises other's willingness to help. For example, the author *"... badly needed to establish a grip on the unions under communist control to stop their political strikes"*, so he reached out to Australia's Ministry of Labour secretary to help set up a court for compulsory arbitration to keep tempers in check (p.324).

### POLITICAL STRATEGY PART 1: Place your goal into perspective

Analyse, then build an overall plan that will bring about your core mission.

* **First, know yourself via your resources**: Assess your alliances with respect to your enemy's. For example, the author's initial allies were limited to the English-educated and the Malays, against pro-communists with greater conviction and numbers.

  - **Use tactics to change the balance in your favour**: The author simultaneously weakened his enemy and strengthened his allies by poaching enemy members as a lawyer who helped at any hour, later taking them in *"...like recruiting police cadets in mafia territory, a hazardous business"* (p.173-174).

* **Create a trusted circle**: The author pushed for splitting the PAP to purge and prevent alliances tainted with corruption.

  *"We decided to lift the party whip and let all vote as they wished. We needed volunteers, not conscripts, for the nasty fight ahead"* (p.375).

* **Know your enemy**: Identify the alliances, resources, strengths, and thoughts of your enemy (p.173). Of the communists, the author learned:
  - They made themselves into living mythologies of self-sacrifice, like leaders sleeping on desks spartan-style, to inspire the masses to emulate them (p.254).
  - If Singapore gained independence before merging with Malaya, the communists would never relinquish that sovereignty (p.363). So the author did not pursue independence until after the merger, and sought to defeat them before it.
  - As long as "...Chinese middle schools were churning out bright and ambitious graduates whom the political system excluded good jobs in the public and private sectors, the [pro-communists] would have a steady flow of recruits" (p.254). So this needed to be addressed.
  
* **Leverage uneasily changed natures**: The author knew the communists would never disarm as they always want the capacity of force when democratic methods failed to win them power. The Emergency Regulations constitutionally inhibited the pro-communists from cowing civilians into silence with terrorist acts. So the author ensured those regulations stayed in place (p.192, 197).

* **Evaluate you and your enemy's relative positions**: Then determine the victory conditions. The author saw his allies as democratic socialists challenging pro-communists, with victory dependent on bringing:
  1. reasonable living standards
  2. effective administration

* **Deduce your enemy's strategy**: Then outscope it. For example, the Plen wanted to abolish the Internal Security Council to give pro-communists freedom to act. But the author knew only the British could revoke such measures as they controlled Singapore's sovereignty and military bases. He anticipated once the Plen realised this, the Plen's next move would be to provoke Chinese chauvinism. So the author pre-emptively pushed for merger into Malaysia before Singapore was overtaken by pro-communists (p.360).

### POLITICAL STRATEGY PART 2: Stay alive

Stay alive to keep your mission alive by positioning yourself as more useful to your enemies alive than dead. 

* **Build a reputation for non-violent mediation**: The author's skill in this contributed to keeping him alive. For example, the author helped a previously violent labour union's strike on the condition they kept it peaceful. The drop in violence made him visibly useful to both sides, and improved his public perception (p.210-211).

* **Proactively discourage the use of killshots against you**: You do not need to disarm an enemy. Sometimes just taking away their willingness to use their arms will suffice. The Plen had elimination squads which he could have used to kill the author and the PAP.
  - The author was aware of these squads.
  - The Plan knew of the author's awareness.
  - On the night of a massive operation to round up the pro-communists, the author sent the media his amnesty offer to pro-communist leader Lim Chin Siong on the premise that Lim never sought to deceive the author on his political leanings. 
  - This diffused the Plen's willingness to use the elimination squads in retaliation, because the author's death after such a public demonstration of honour would serve as evidence of pro-communist indecency (p.473).

* **Foster stability by keeping your enemy alive but down**: Do not completely wipe out your enemy. Having a small and controlled enemy presence helps your constituency in self-regulating their ideological stability. It also shows your ability to grant mercy, which seeds hope for the enemy to reciprocate that mercy, in case the tables later turn on you (p.251, 462).

* **Thank those who save you**: The author wrote to Head who was leaving his position, and thanking him for dissuading the Tunku and the Ultras from killing the author, which he had learned later on (p.656).

### CAMPAIGNING

Deliver your message to gain support for your goals.

* **Speak the language of the people**: If they have multiple languages, speak those languages to better reach them. It helps convince constituents of your political position, and your visible comfortability in being with people from different races (p.487).

* **Adapt your speaking methodology to the medium**: From a mock TV interview, the author learned to:
  - Not use mass rally gestures and vocals on TV, as they come across negatively.
  - Always look into the camera, and avoid looking down at notes.
  - Never cover your mouth or nose with your hand whilst speaking.
  - Always lean forward in your chair, because leaning backward is slovenly.
  - Be natural, direct, and yourself (p.492).

* **Be strong in public speeches**: Speak eloquently, passionately, and with rhetoric, whilst avoiding being feeble, tepid, dull, and convictionless (p.186). When speaking in an unmastered secondary language, you can still earn respect by showing you are working hard at it (p.300). Be inclusive. For example, despite crowd hostility, the author pushed to make them feel Singapore was as much their achievement as it was his (p.489).

* **Emotions may win short-term, but logic wins long-term campaigns**: Garnering people's support through revolutionary zeal does not last because people inevitably cast it aside for the realism needed to raise families (p.255).

* **Use short and truthfully sharp retorts in debates**: Turn your enemy's argument against to expose hypocrisy. For example, a Democratic Party opponent claimed the author did not represent the Chinese voter because he could not read or write Chinese. The author countered that his opponent could not read or write Tamil and Malay, so that opponent was unsuited to represent other constituents by his own logic (p.183).

* **Sliced ginseng for long campaigns**: Excessive speaking can lead to a hoarse voice. The author recommends leaving a slice of ginseng between the cheek and gums to ease the pain better than lozenges do, and he did so on his campaign tours (p.489).

* **Not every rally needs to instill hope**: In one rally, the author dampened the crowd's hope to prepare them against communist pushes for "freedom" which really were a subversion of Singapore to communism (p.308).

* **Go for the small and easy wins first**: Move quickly during post-election popularity, like holding well-publicised campaign to clean city streets and beaches with everyone including ministers toiling with their hands and soiling their clothes to set higher standards in civic consciousness (p.322).

* **Be bold. Be the precedence.**: Face problems head on and expose efforts to sweep them under the rug. The author's final parliamentary speech in Kuala Lumpur during the *Eve of Independence* was his most significant:
  - He spoke in Malayan without a script, about crucially sensitive issues in a rational down-to-earth manner on improving the economic lot of Malaya's rural communities.
  - The audience included backbencher Malayan MPs representing those constituencies largely ignored by the central government.
  - The stranger's gallery was filled with civilian rural Malays who also agreed with the author (p.613-615).

  _"The chamber was very quiet and nobody stirred. The ministers of the central government sunk down so low in their seats that only their foreheads could be seen over the desk in front of them. The backbenchers were spellbound. They could understand every word. That was the turning point. They perceived Lee as a dangerous man who could one day be the prime minister of Malaysia"_ - Othman Wok (p.613-614)

### DIPLOMACY

* **Build an international reputation for insight**: Exchange your insight for another's action. The author had an unreported meeting with Australian prime minister Sir Robert Menzies acknowledging his influence. The author offered perspectives that others were too involved to see, in exchange for asking Menzies' to explain to the Tunku the long-term future of the pro-communalist path (p.595-597).

* **Maintain relations such that confidential diplomatic dialogue remains possible**: Such frank exchanges may be needed to prevent disaster, as was the case during the *Racial Riots* between the author with Keng Swee, and Tunku with Razak (p.591).

* **Stay afloat of elections in other countries**: This gives you a chance for pre-emptive action, like pursuading the potentially new government to honour the commitments of the current one (p.573-574).

* **Be timely courteous, but not at the compromise of your core mission**: Out of courtesy, the author notified Lord Head of Malaysia and Singapore's intention to split, half an hour before the independence bill was introduced in parliament (p.630-631). The author also sent encoded messages to notify the prime ministers of the Commonwealth countries that protected them against Indonesia, with estimated time of decoding being after the split's finalisation (p.646).

* **Be mindful of your post-conflict actions**: Do not rub your enemy's nose in the dirt after your victory, especially when reconciliation is needed for long term positive neighbourship (p.520).

### NEGOTIATION

* **Study the negotiation tactics of your counterpart**: The author notes the British characteristic to avoid saying "there will be no changes", and instead gradually erode the other person's point of view (p.590).

* **Be careful of underhanded tactics during proximity talks**: Proximity talks are where a 3rd party mediates negotiations between two parties. A mediator tactic to wear down both for easier negotiation is to run discussions late into the night with little food, other than hard liquor. Decline the alcohol, and source food separately (p.480).

* **Ensure every deal is at least fair to avoid being exploited**: The author was excellent at this. For example, during Singapore vs Malaysia negotiations:

  - The Tunku wanted control of Singapore's defence, in case Singapore turned to communism. But the author countered if Singapore paid for defence via federal tax, then the tax should not be allowed without Singaporean representation in federal parliament (p.586).

  - Australian High Commissioner Tom Critchley negotiated with the author to close the PAP branches in the Malaysian peninsula in return for having 2 ministers in Malaysian parliament. But the author countered the UMNO should also withdraw its branches from Singapore, otherwise Singaporeans would be under constant threat (p.591).

* **Use cold feet to your advantage**: After a negotiated agreement is in motion, if your counterpart gets cold feet, one possible approach is to not force them to adhere to the plan, but let them come to the conclusion themselves there is no other way. You can do this by showing willingness to approach the problem how your counterpart likes, let them think aloud alternatives, shoot down each on why they are not fair or realistic until they run out, then say you have no ideas either (p.623-634).



## POLITICAL TACTICS

* **Be the lesser of two evils for your enemies**: A large part of the author's strategy depended on this tactic, effective because there were only 2 options for the enemy. 
  - Stage 1 pro-communists: Their 2 enemies were the anti-communist nationalists and the PAP. So they sided and merged with the PAP. 
  - Stage 1.5: Combined, both would be crushed together by pro-communalist Malaya, so the author split the party. 
  - Stage 2 pro-communalists: Their 2 enemies were the pro-communists and the PAP. So they sided with the PAP to crush the pro-communists.
  - Stage 3 colonialist allies: Their 2 enemies were Indonesia and an unstable Malaysia with Singapore. So they reluctantly accepted the Singapore split, to keep Indonesia from pressing further.

* **Study different tactics, including failed ones**: Because sometimes tactic effectiveness is based on timing and context. For example, in pre-war India, constitutional methods of passive resistance against colonial authority took decades to work, but it worked faster for the PAP because of the pro-communist threat (p.211).

* **Disguise your true intentions when building leverage**: At British suggestion, the author petitioned the Tunku for permission to tour Africa with the overt objective of winning support of Malaysia against Indonesia, but had the subvert objective of making the author internationally renown and harder for the Tunku to lock up later (p.522-525).

* **Build a good sense for timing events to favour your strategy**: Because seemingly positive actions can have negative effects depending on when you do them. So think ahead for implications along different timing contexts. For example:

  - The Tunku wanted Singaporean communists to be cleaned up before the merger, but the author agreed only on condition that the British had responsibility for security during it, and for it to happen after the referendum so that the author himself wouldn't be viewed as arresting them to win Singapore (p.426).

  - The author also did not want to detain the pro-communists pre-maturely, because doing that would make it look like the PAP were harming Chinese-speaking workers. The PAP wanted the communists to build some degree of strength to self-demonstrate their harm to the economy (p.349).

* **Prevent propaganda from undermining your efforts**: The media is a powerful means to facilitate malicious falsehoods, such as the systematic exploitation of Malayan media to agitate the federation-wide bloodbaths in the *Racial Riots* against ethnic Chinese (p.554-558 & 562). 

  - Media's primary advantage is overwhelming the public domain, exhausting opposition time and resources to counter each falsehood. Therefore, take large-scale efficient preventative steps, like legally implementing the "right of reply" against media.

  - Leverage existing legislation against media threats to your objectives. For example, the author sought public support to arrest newspaper stakeholders trying to strain relations between Malaya and Singapore leading up to merger, based on subversion under the Preservation of Public Security Ordinance (p.299).

  - If falsehoods are published in a language only a single leveragable ethnic group can read, then counter it by ensuring readily available translations for other ethnic groups to expose such racial campaigns, and to delegate responsibility to ethnic groups for the correcting of each other's manipulation (p.551, 585).

  - Allow 3rd parties to expose illegitimate media claims, like how Australian and New Zealander leaders wrote to Singaporean newspapers to defend the author from false speech allegations in disparaging Malaysian leaders (p.597-598).

* **Counter propaganda with transparency and a powerful reason**: Propaganda's strength lies in the willful ignorance of people. So give the people a reason to want to know the truth. The author's "Battle for Merger" national radio speeches revealed the PAP's story with the pro-communists. He diplomatically credited the pro-communist strength and conviction, but revealed their underground and the existence of the Plen. Chinese-speaking fence-sitters were convinced the PAP were winning by the fear that siding with pro-communists would lead to federal punishment (p.393-398).

  - His less than half hour speeches over Radio Singapore were broadcasted from 13th September to 9th October, 3 times a week, each time in 3 languages, 2 of them in Malay and Mandarin (p.394).
  
  - Cheong Yip Seng (later Straits Times editor-in-chief) was a schoolboy and saw these speeches as an eye-opener, because the events were happening as the speeches aired, leaving the people in suspense and anxious for the next. "A master storyteller was at work. But this was not fiction. This was life and death for Singaporeans" (p.398).

* **Make seemingly large but long-term practically small concessions**: This demonstrates that you are able to be negotiated with and can earn favour. For example, the Tunku was pleased when the author proposed the appointment of a Malayan as one of Singapore's 2 senators in Federal parliament (p.517).

* **Taking a deliberate step backwards if it earns you 2 steps forward in your mission**: For example, following the PAP's initial election win, the author anticipated PAP pro-communists would obstruct PAP non-communists from policy implementation. So his closest colleagues signed a public statement to not pursue the top PAP positions on the basis of lacking moral right, as they knew the danger pro-communists were for Singaporeans. It also forced PAP pro-communists to take responsibility for their own corruption, which inevitably happened soon after (p.268-271). He did this before the pro-communists grew whilst hiding behind the non-communist member's integrity.

* **Drain the human resources of an underground network**: For underground networks with a grassroots public united front, take out the front's leaders. Then keep a pulse on whether those vacancies are replaced by those from the underground. It signals whether or not there are any cadres left willing to go out into the open (p.472).

* **Remove prominent leaders publicly supporting the enemy**: After winning elections shortly after merger, the author made moves against prominent figures who espoused communist rhetoric and believed their wealth and standing gave them immunity, like how he sought to cancel the citizenship of Nanyang University's founder, Tan Lark Sye, on the basis of electoral interference (p.511).

* **Anticipate and set up traps**: For example, Singapore's Chinese Chamber of Commerce organised a rally on what they viewed as Japanese insincerity over WWII reparation negotiations. The author agreed to speak at the rally, but also knew the pro-communists would infiltrate the crowds to cause trouble. So he prepared Special Branch, 6000 deployed police, and regiment troops to shine spotlights on crowd sections that got rowdy, with TV cameramen ready to take close-ups to identify ringleaders. The troublemakers realised their jeering's ineffectiveness, which made the author a brave and resourceful leader to the public (p.496-497).

* **Every strength is its own weakness**: Every strength has a crutch, attack that crutch to disable that strength. For example:

  - The communists made a constant big deal of the nomenclature that seemed like Singaporeans would not become Malaysian citizens for merger. So the author negotiated with Tunku via Razak to amend this, though its actual success turned out to be due to British and Australian pressure via the United Nations (p.438-439, 444).

  - During the *Racial Riots* one pro-communalist tactic was to trigger riots in a location fiercely enough until the Chinese counter-attacked, then the police and army would clobber the Chinese, but not the Malays involved. The author leveraged the "one location" weakness of this tactic, by linking communications such that if a riot triggered in one city, riots counter-triggered in other cities, converting the pro-communalists intimidation tactic into a double-edged sword that could tear Malaysia apart (p.602).

* **Divide and conquer**: For example, the PAP members Chin Chye and Raja were known to the author as those least likely to sign the documents needed for Singapore to split from Malaysia. So to prevent them from meeting beforehand and stiffening their resolve not to, he had the documents signed by everyone else first, then had them driven separately to a location to inform them of the split and decide whether or not to sign (p.640).

### APPLIED EXTERNAL GOVERNMENT POLICY

* **Pursue governance through knowledge and competence**: Do not apply "...misguided policies based on half-digested theories of socialism and redistribution of wealth". The author observed in his tour of post-colonial Africa that countries failed where they incompetently pursued such ideologies (p.538-539).

* **Ensure the national budget is broad sweeping of problems**: Do not focus on just the large townships. It should also benefit rural area farmers, especially if the bulk of the population lives there (p.519).

### INTERNAL GOVERNMENT POLICY & OPERATIONS

Singapore's PAP leaders and ministers were unlike other politicians from around the world. They were not "pleasure-loving" nor self-enriching (p.656). This self-discipline and pursuit for highest integrity and competence possible led to the PAP's effectiveness in bringing so much for their people. 

* **Control your public perception from genuine disciplined effort**: Have a "...self-imposed and much publicised rule that ministers should cut down drastically on attendance at non-official functions" which are otherwise perceived as self-serving cocktail party social climbing, which loses the people's trust (p.382).

* **Make the fight against government corruption constitutional and practical**: By introducing a bill to give wide powers to combat corruption such that offenders could be charged and convicted in court, and create an agency to keep the country clean of corruption. In Singapore, this agency is the Corrupt Practices Investigation Bureau (p.346).

* **Put policies in place to prevent your central governing committe from being corrupted by newcomers**: Constitutionally define 2 classes of party membership, ordinary and cadre, where only cadre members are approved by the existing cadres, to prevent the party from being captured by an external group. This is based on Rome's election method for the Pope (p.287).

* **When in dire straits, have everyone lead by example**: If needing to set a national tone for thrift and financial discipline, begin with cutting ministerial salaries to show the nation thst for the public good, those in government would share in the hardships and joys equally with the people (p.318).

* **Get the most out of skip meetings**: Only involve people in meetings who understand the perspectives of all stakeholders involved. For example, the author saw that a newly appointed director didn't have the subtlety to understand communist tactics and strategy. So the author had meetings with two senior officers who were more shrewd than their director (p.329).

### NATION-BUILDING AND COMMUNITY DEVELOPMENT

* **Provide community centres that encourage participation in nation-building**: Provide many community centres in the city and rural areas for education and recreation supervised by Social Welfare "...to give people something positive to do, and get them lined up on the side of law and order" (p.324).

* **Build a place for the unemployed to have utility**: Like a Works Brigade to take the unemployed, "...put them in semi-military uniforms, house them in wooden barracks and teach them farming, road building, bricklaying and construction work - generally to put some discipline into them and ... get them off the streets" (p.324).

* **Make clear and public your expectations for people's personal standards**: For example, if the nation gets slovenly and littered following an event or disaster, call a meeting for all public health officers with full press and TV coverage, urging them and the people to restore cleanliness (p.579).

* **Introduce a brass program from primary to university school**: The author was inspired by a Socialist International celebration in Brussels, seeing that music was a necessary part of nation-building (p.570).

## DISCUSSON

In another book review I wrote how there is:

_"...the misplaced tendency to judge the biographee as a person, rather than the biography as a book."_

Politics inherently attracts character doubt, so I will address that with a decision tree at 3 key junctures from this volume.

![Decision tree](/src/assets/readings/the-singapore-story-volume-1-by-lee-kuan-yew-decision-tree.png)

From this it becomes more obvious that the author was in a no-choice situation, as is the case for many legends of old. Their conviction preordains their path.

But these thoughts did cross my mind:

* Is it true that between the two, only Goh Keng Swee thought Singapore was better off independent after merger, necessitating his working behind the author's back to start the separation?

* Or did the author anticipate his country needed to suffer a genuine yet failed attempt at merger (and all the bloodshed it would involve) so that the people would fully accept they were on their own, then use that to build the morale needed for them to take responsibility for their own country?

Knowing the author's practicality, insight, and vigilance, makes it difficult to believe the claim it took him a year to realise that the Tunku would not give up his pro-communalist alliance partners in favour of a "Malaysian Malaysia" (p.516).

I believe somewhere deep down, he knew the "Malaysian Malaysia" project would fail. But if it were pressed for hard and fast, it would have the minimal overall casualties compared to any other pathway and without subordinating Singaporeans to another nation. Its failure would also prevent anyone from trying to merge Singapore into another nation again, thus leaving Singaporeans free to choose their own destiny. Though that could all just be projection on my part.

Or perhaps the author truly hoped in the Malaysian Malaysia project that much? It would have made for the most ideal alliance from Singapore's perspective, to have an ethnically equal neighbour than an ethnically-dominated one. In the face of what most others blindly call impossible, one must believe in the **possible** and overturn its improbability with willpower and effort. Even if ultimately, the author and his allies were unable to overturn that improbability at the time.

At this stage, I will never know the answer.
But I suppose it does not matter now.
Either way, the author is unquestionably Singapore's father, and he did what was best for its people.

This ends the "judging the biographee instead of the book" section of this discussion.



## REVIEW

This book is written to-the-point and matter-of-factly, letting the information stand on its own in a slightly dry yet event-filled way with a heft of authenticity. The rare moments of colourfully described individuals and moments are an oasis. For example, during the secret meeting to split Singapore from Malaysia, Keng Swee urged it be a fait accompli, first by granting Singapore's independence via constitutional amendments then have all readings take place that day, and in response, "Razak was greatly amused and said that perhaps PAP tactics were the best" (p.630).

It also demonstrates an integrity and character benchmark that is practically achievable for politicians, based not just on the author himself, but also on those around him. Intelligent, courageous, civil-minded, hardworking, humble, realistic, systematic, witty, hopeful, persevering, and unimpeded by negative traits like self-serving, naive, ideologically corrupt, and chauvinistic. Such qualities are well encompassed in a proverb from PAP member Yaacob bin Mohamed: 

*"A hen lays one egg, the whole village hears her cackles; a turtle lays eggs by the hundred, not a sound is heard"* (p.300-301).

Implying the PAP brought many benefits to the Singaporean people, but the PAP do not boast about it because they have the discipline to work toward constant results, and the humility to expect such effort of themselves.

I will admit, comparing the high standard of the post-Independence PAP member, to present day media's negative portrayals of politicians, and those whose shortcomings are self-apparent, does make those founding PAP members an object of envy. Though I understand it would be better to frame them as a goal to reach in terms of character, competence, and accomplishment.

Whilst not explicitly said in the book, I will recount what the author was once quoted to have said, which summarises what this story's heroes fought for:

**"Do not let the system become corrupt."**

Singapore's story is a historical miracle of South East Asia, and this book shows it was forged by the country's brave, clever, and intrinsically good people.

This was by far the hardest book review I have written, initially because of my aversion to politics, but largely because of the sheer volume of lessons and anecdotes I wanted to include. But I realise the lack of tact and brevity in doing that, so I cut out an enormous amount.

Having only read Volume I, I already wholeheartedly agree with Charlie Munger when he said:

*"If you will make a study of the life and work of Lee Kuan Yew, you will find one of the most interesting and instructive political stories written in the history of mankind... and you will learn a lot that will be useful in your whole life"* (YAPSS Archive, YouTube channel)

For that reason, if you only read one political autobiography in your lifetime, I'd recommend this one. As an added bonus, you will find an amusing anecdote about an unwise use of orange Fanta.

Now, to find the second volume...
